It is well-known and widely recognized in the fatigue community that a material's internal structure significantly influences its fatigue characteristics. Although the SN diagram and Paris' law are extremely popular in fatigue design, these empirical models do not explicitly account for the material's internal structure. The goal of microstructure-sensitive fatigue modeling is to provide a bottom-up simulation framework that can predict emerging deformation and damage characteristics based on a material's given microstructure. Applications of such a framework include, but are not limited to, the integrated design of materials and parts for safety-critical applications, accelerated material qualification, and prognosis.

Imagine there are two identical-looking engineering components, such as turbine discs. Both are produced via forging, but one undergoes additional heat treatment. As an engineer, you intuitively know that although the two discs appear the same, their mechanical performance will differ due to their different internal states. If you perform a stress analysis using FEA software without considering the initial internal state, the prediction will be significantly off. Therefore, it is essential to accurately initialize the internal state within the simulation framework. But what is the internal state, and how do we initialize it?

In simple stress analysis, the internal state refers to residual stress. In more complex models, the internal state can include factors such as dislocation density and intragranular misorientation.

Our group focuses on modeling residual stress at different material length scales, incorporating it into simulation frameworks, identifying optimal locations for residual stress measurements in engineering components, and designing new technologies to induce location-specific residual stress for targeted mechanical performance.

If you are new to the concept of residual stress, you may read the papers by Withers and Bhadeshia (paper 1, paper 2). For a deeper understanding, refer to the paper cited below the image. You are always welcome to contact our group leader if you want to discuss this topic further.

Have you heard the quote, "All models are wrong, but some are useful"? This idea, often attributed to British statistician George E. P. Box, highlights that no model is completely accurate because it is a simplification of reality; some information is always lost. If nothing were lost, the model would be an exact copy, not a model.

In our group, we approach modeling and simulation with this humility. We not only predict values, such as stress concentration and fatigue life, but also quantify the associated uncertainty, which can be thought of as a prediction interval or a probability distribution.

In our modeling and simulation activities, we consider the following questions:

  1. Can we precisely measure or calibrate the model's parameter values? If not, can we determine the probability distributions for these parameters?
  2. Knowing the model parameters are random variables, can we propagate their distributions through simulations to characterize the probability distribution of the output variables?
  3. Among multiple mathematical models describing the same physical phenomenon, can we identify which model leads to the least prediction uncertainty?
We address these questions using various probabilistic methods. We take uncertainty quantification and propagation seriously, aiming to translate the tools and frameworks developed in our lab for practical applications.
